A Letter to You

Why I'm Doing This

I'm not a doctor. I'm not a biologist. I'm a data scientist and engineer who builds things with AI.

I started this project with a simple question: what if the tools we use every day — LLMs, autonomous agents, data pipelines — could be useful to the HD research community?

There are brilliant scientists working on Huntington's Disease. uniQure's AMT-130 gene therapy demonstrated 75% disease slowing in a Phase I/II trial (Nature, 2025). Novartis has used generative AI to design candidate compounds (Drug Target Review). Unlearn is building digital twins from Enroll-HD patient data (Newswire, Feb 2026). The science is moving.

I wanted to explore whether open-source AI tools could contribute something useful alongside this work — even in a small way. Not to replace researchers, but to help surface patterns across papers, generate starting-point hypotheses, and make the research landscape easier to navigate.

So I built this research dashboard and a set of AI agents.

In our first experiment, we fed 22 recent PubMed abstracts to Llama 3.1 8B (an open-source LLM running on an NVIDIA Jetson). The AI extracted molecular targets and suggested drug repurposing ideas. Some suggestions were interesting starting points — for example, it flagged the IL-6 pathway for neuroinflammation. Others were clearly limited — it suggested Riluzole without knowing it had already been tested in HD trials. We published both outcomes because transparency about what AI can and can't do matters more than looking impressive.

To be clear: none of our AI-generated hypotheses have been reviewed by domain experts or validated experimentally. They are computational starting points, not findings. They need wet-lab testing, expert review, and clinical validation before they mean anything. We publish them openly so the community can evaluate, critique, and build on them if they see potential.

This is not a medical product. I can't cure anything. What I can do is build tools, share data, and ask questions. If any of this is useful to someone who knows more than I do — that's the whole point.

Everything is open source. The code is MIT-licensed. The data comes from public sources (PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, HDBuzz). The AI runs locally on consumer hardware.

The art of the possible starts with someone deciding to try.

JR
Ravinder
Data Scientist + Engineer

The Vision

rocket_launch
Innovative like Musk

First principles. Why does drug discovery take 10 years? Question every assumption. If nobody's running autonomous AI agents on HD research, that's not a reason not to — it's the reason to start.

Bold like Jensen

Push the limits. Don't be afraid to try things nobody has tried. "Tokens are the new electricity" — apply that energy to HD research.

science
Rigorous like Karpathy

Autoresearch mindset. Run the experiments. Let the data speak. Ship code, not slides. 100 experiments overnight is better than 1 perfect plan.

school
Educational like Andrew Ng

Make it accessible. Lead with the question. Show the work. If a beginner can't follow your experiment, rewrite it.

favorite
Grounded like Fei-Fei Li

AI is not inherently good or bad — it's up to us to decide how we use it. Keep it human-centered. This is about patients and families, not technology for its own sake. The AI is a tool in service of people. Ground every claim in evidence. When you don't know, say so. When the AI gets it wrong, publish that too.

Problem → Idea → Iterate

That's the whole method. See a gap, build something, publish what you learn, repeat. Forever.
