A Letter to You

Why I'm Doing This

I'm not a doctor. I'm not a biologist. I'm a data scientist and engineer who builds things with AI.

I started this project with a simple question: what if the tools we use every day — LLMs, autonomous agents, data pipelines — could help accelerate research for diseases like Huntington's?

Here's what I found: there are brilliant scientists working on HD. Companies like uniQure built a gene therapy that slows the disease by 75%. Novartis is using generative AI to design millions of compounds. Unlearn is building digital twins from patient data. The science is moving.

But I also found gaps. Nobody was running autonomous AI research agents on HD. Nobody had an open-source drug repurposing scanner for it. Nobody was pulling PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, and social media into a single, auto-refreshing research dashboard. These aren't hard problems for a data scientist — they're just problems nobody had pointed AI at yet.

So I built this. In one night.

Our AI analyzed 46 papers and generated drug repurposing hypotheses. Some were good (Tocilizumab for neuroinflammation scored 80/100). Some were wrong (it suggested Riluzole, already tested with modest results). We published both — because honesty about what AI can and can't do matters more than looking smart.

This is not a medical product. I can't cure anything. What I can do is build tools that surface patterns, generate ideas, and make research more accessible. If one hypothesis from our AI agent catches the eye of one researcher and leads to one experiment — that's worth it.

Everything here is open source. Use it, build on it, improve it. The code is MIT-licensed. The data comes from public sources. The AI runs on a $1,000 edge device in my home office.

The art of the possible starts with someone deciding to try.

JR
Ravinder
Data Scientist + Engineer

Stay humble, stay human. This is about patients and families. The AI is a tool, not the hero. Ground every claim in evidence. When you don't know, say so.
